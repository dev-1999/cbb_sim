What is Rick Pitino Doing in Greece?

Nick Flynt
27/12/2018
CourtSide, Featured
1

Apparently, hes there to coach Panathinaikos.

If I didnt know better, I would have said going on an extended trip of the Greek Isles, or something along those lines. That would seem to be a more logical guess. But were leaving the land of logic, sports fans.

Instead, Pitino, coming off a number of embarrassingscandals at his college job at the University of Louisville, is going to come into a…tough situation…as PAOs head coach. And hell be working for one of the craziest owners in all of sports in the person of Dimitrios Pavlos Giannakopoulos. If you follow the above links, youll see that those scandals involved a sex-for-pay scandal, an FBI probe, and before any of that Pitino was also the victim of an extortion attempt in relation to an extramarital liaison in a restaurant bathroom. All of that is salacious, titillating, what have you — but PAO fans might be even more scandalized when they hear about his infamous run as coach/president of the Boston Celtics. The headline at SBNations CelticsBlog.com?

Rick Pitino was the absolute worst

If were just talking about the on-court stuff, Pitino is known partly for his match-up press defense. You dont see full-court press defenses at the highest levels of basketball for a reason. The players are more skilled all over the court, decision-making is better — in general, youre liable to commit more fouls and give-up more easy baskets running a press than you are to produce quick turnovers and layups for your own team. Pitino has alsobeen described in a way that gives you the feeling he likes an old school, Hard Man Practice.

Its very rare when that mentality works at the pro level — take a look at Jim Boylen with the Chicago Bulls lately, for instance.

In terms of the changes Pitino should make to get PAO on track…they need offense, obviously. The go-to cliche of any new coach coming in, when talking offense, is that the team needs to run more. When you have a team like PAO that can defend (currently top-3 in defensive efficiency in EuroLeague, by my count) but is shooting less than 30% from 3…yeah, running more couldnt hurt. I also have a slight obsession with European coaches as a whole playing their best players more — if Im Pitino, I come in and immediately play my favorite guys as close to 35, 36 minutes per game as they can handle.

Rick Pitino with Calathes and Lekavicius (photo from: https://paobc.gr/page.ashx?pid=2&aid=54126)

By no means do I want to imply that success is impossible. Pitino will, unless Im vastly overestimating a coach who has been very successful on-court at the college level, be able to come in and make adjustments to his system and philosophy based on what does and doesnt fly at the highest level in Europe. Note that I say able — Im a little more worried about if hell be willing to be flexible — this is a 66-year-old man who has just spent 16 or so years coaching at the same university, remember. I dont know if thats a recipe for flexibility of ideology. And while I have already said that the situation at PAO is a tough one, it isnt disastrous. Pitino isnt coming in to run things for 1-13 Darafaka, here. But changing horses midstream, as the old saying goes, is rarely a good idea even in the best of situations. PAO isnt meeting their owners high expectations. Pitino has no European experience. His professional coaching experience, which came to an end almost two decades ago, finished on an infamously poor note.

He, much like Larry Brown, is coming to Europe because his last coaching job ended in scandal.

So, you could say I dont have high hopes for anyone involved in this move. Im mostly trying to figure out what motivated the brain trust at PAO to go out and get Rick Pitino. The only thing I can imagine is that someone mentioned to them that a big coaching name was available (maybe at a cheap price, if Pitino is really desperate to get back into coaching?), and they jumped at the opportunity without thinking it through — which, coincidentally, is something very characteristic of Giannakopoulos. (Case in Point #1, Case in Point #2)

Good luck to all involved — as someone who isnt a fan of Panathinaikos, Im very intrigued to see how this experiment goes.

  1. Kevin O'Brien 27/12/2018

    I am also intrigued by this move by PAO. I think there are some interesting things to note regarding Pitino as a head coach

    1.) I think one thing everyone mentions with Pitino is the press, and rightfully so. He earned his coaching chops with his full court pressure defense at Providence, Kentucky and Louisville. But Pitino doesn’t live and die by the press. Many of his Louisville teams, especially later in his career, rarely ran the press at all. But, Pitino has always been known running for fast paced teams with big men who can do multiple things, including bringing the ball up and shooting the three (Pitino was ahead of the game on this, as he utilized Antoine Walker as a do everything post player). I think Pitino can spark this offense and will get more mileage out of them offensively, which I never felt Pascual could really full do outside of Calathes. Pitino’s teams traditionally shot the three ball well and often, and I imagine their 3 point struggles despite having guys like Lojeski, Langford, and Thomas (a big man who will thrive under Pitino’s style as a coach) was a big reason why they went after Pitino in the first place.

    2.) Pitino was a terrible pro coach because he had trouble handling stars and was an awful evaluator of pro talent. Pitino was never a great recruiter either in college. He never had really highly ranked classes at Louisville or Kentucky. But he’s always been good at getting the most of what he’s got, and getting guys to play beyond what is expected. Just look at Louisville players in the NBA…not a really high success rate, which is more of a credit to Pitino as a coach. PAO is an ideal fit: he’s not in charge of acquiring talent, and the EL tends to be more of a coaches league like college than a players league like the NBA. If Obradovic and Blatt can find success despite abrasive styles, then it isn’t hard to imagine Pitino fitting and succeeding either.

    3.) I think this is a temporary gamble by both squads who have a lot to gain in the long term. Olympiacos hired Blatt and for the most part they have continued to hum. I’m sure the fact that a coach for a rival squad with NBA pedigree motivated PAO to find someone with a similar background to head this PAO team. I like how you brought up how European coaches tend to play much deeper rotations, which I don’t understand considering how important every game is in the EL. Blatt actually has played shallower rotations since coming back to Europe from the NBA and it has been successful, especially in his last stint with Dacka, who have been a shitshow since he left. And case in point: four players for Olympiacos are averaging over 24 minutes a game. Pao? Only 1 (Calathes who is averaging 30). Expect this to change under Pitino, which I think may be more of an advantage, especially with so many American players on this roster who like playing more extended minutes as they see in the NCAA and Pro level back in the States.

    As for Pitino, its obvious he wants to coach still. He’s been campaigning ever since the summer that he would come back for any kind of job. Unfortunately, the stain on him is too much for any college or pro team to take a risk on him. For Pitino, Pao provides a good situation: the team is talented and deep, they have the resources to succeed, and really nobody is expecting him to be there long term. If Pitino can spark this PAO squad to a strong finish and maybe a Final Four appearance, American colleges will notice, especially mid-majors who are looking for anything to turn around their team.

    As you stated, this will be an intereting development. Even though it was absurd in the moment, and came out of nowhere, I think there are more pros than cons to this hire for both parties involved. Nonetheless, I look forward to see how the team meshes under Pitino, and how patient this PAO ownership will be with him over the second half of the EL season.
